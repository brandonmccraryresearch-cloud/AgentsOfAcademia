2. Mathematical Preliminaries (Self‑Contained)

To make the document accessible, we summarise the essential tools from the IRH framework. Full details and proofs can be found in the main IRH manuscript, but all definitions needed for the theorems below are given here.

2.1 Slowly Varying Envelope Approximation (SVEA)

For processes with energy $E \ll E_P = \hbar\Omega_P$, the envelope $\psi$ satisfies

\left|\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial\tau}\right| \ll \Omega_P|\psi|,\qquad 
|\nabla^2\psi| \ll \frac{\Omega_P^2}{c^2}|\psi|.

Thus $\psi$ varies on scales much larger than the lattice spacing $a_0$ and the fundamental period $1/\Omega_P$.

2.2 Coarse‑Graining Map

Let $\{u_n^\mu\}_{n\in\Lambda_{D_4}}$ be the discrete displacement field. For a continuum point $x$, choose a cell $\mathcal{C}(x)$ of linear size $L \gg a_0$ centred at $x$ containing $N_{\rm cell} = (L/a_0)^4$ lattice sites. Define the coarse‑grained displacement

\bar u^\mu(x) = \frac{1}{N_{\rm cell}}\sum_{n\in\mathcal{C}(x)} u_n^\mu.

The strain tensor is

\varepsilon_{\mu\nu}(x) = \frac12\bigl(\partial_\mu \bar u_\nu(x) + \partial_\nu \bar u_\mu(x)\bigr).

Error bound (proved in IRH §V.4): For the $D_4$ lattice,

\|\bar u^\mu - u^\mu_{\text{exact}}\| \le \frac{a_0^2}{12}\,R_{\text{max}},

where $R_{\text{max}}$ is the maximum curvature scale. This ensures that the continuum approximation is excellent for all astrophysical spacetimes.

2.3 Interference Metric

From the strain tensor, the emergent metric is

g_{\mu\nu}(x) = \eta_{\mu\nu} + 2\varepsilon_{\mu\nu}(x),\qquad \eta_{\mu\nu}={\rm diag}(-1,+1,+1,+1).

Under the SVEA, the displacement is dominated by the envelope. For the longitudinal (conformal) part of the metric, one finds

\varepsilon_{\mu\nu} \approx \frac{1}{\Omega_P}\,\partial_\mu\partial_\nu S,

leading to the interference metric

\boxed{ g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} + \frac{2}{\Omega_P}\,\partial_\mu\partial_\nu S + \mathcal O\!\left((\partial S)^2,\; a_0^2 R_{\text{max}}\right) }.

Theorem 1 (Lorentzian Signature). If $|\partial^2 S| \ll \Omega_P$, then $g_{\mu\nu}$ is invertible and has signature $(-,+,+,+)$.

Proof sketch. The determinant $\det(g) = -1 + \frac{2}{\Omega_P}\Box S + \mathcal O((\partial S)^2)$. Since $|\Box S| \lesssim |\partial^2 S| \ll \Omega_P$, $\det(g)$ remains negative. Moreover, $g_{00} = -1 + \frac{2}{\Omega_P}\partial_0^2 S$ and $g_{ij}= \delta_{ij} + \frac{2}{\Omega_P}\partial_i\partial_j S$, so the perturbations are small. By continuity, the eigenvalues retain one negative and three positive signs. $\square$

3. The Phase Source and Emergent Gravity

3.1 Derivation of the Source Equation

We now derive the key equation $\Box S = -\frac{4\pi G}{c^4}\rho_{\text{matter}}$ from the lattice action.

The unified IRH Hamiltonian density (IRH §I.6) contains the interaction term

\mathcal{H}_{\text{int}} = \frac{\lambda_3}{2}\,\phi_{\text{ARO}}(\nabla u)^2,

where $\lambda_3 = O(1)$ is the anharmonic coupling. Under the SVEA, the ARO field $\phi_{\text{ARO}} = A\cos(\Omega_P\tau)$ oscillates rapidly, while the envelope $\psi$ is slow. The time‑average of the interaction over a period $2\pi/\Omega_P$ eliminates the fast oscillations, and the leading contribution becomes proportional to $(\nabla S)^2$ multiplied by the matter energy density. In more detail: writing $u = \operatorname{Re}(\psi e^{i\Omega_P\tau})$, we have $(\nabla u)^2 \approx \frac{1}{2}(\nabla\psi)^2 e^{i2\Omega_P\tau} + \text{c.c.} + \frac12 |\nabla\psi|^2$. The oscillatory terms average to zero, while $|\nabla\psi|^2 = (\nabla R)^2 + R^2(\nabla S)^2$. For slowly varying $R$, the dominant term is $R^2(\nabla S)^2$, which is proportional to the matter density $\rho_{\text{matter}}$ (since $R^2$ is related to the defect amplitude). The effective Lagrangian therefore acquires a term

\mathcal{L}_{\text{eff}} \supset \frac{1}{2}(\nabla S)^2 - \frac{4\pi G}{c^4}\,\rho_{\text{matter}}\,S,

where the coefficient $4\pi G/c^4$ is fixed by matching the Newtonian limit (see below). Varying this effective action with respect to $S$ yields the Euler‑Lagrange equation

\nabla^2 S = -\frac{4\pi G}{c^4}\,\rho_{\text{matter}}.

In the relativistic setting, the spatial Laplacian generalises to the d'Alembertian $\Box S$.

The constant of proportionality is determined by the lattice elastic modulus. From IRH §II.4, Newton's constant is

G = \frac{24\,c^2 a_0}{M^*},\qquad M^* = \sqrt{24}\,M_P,\; a_0 = \frac{L_P}{\sqrt{24}}.

Substituting these lattice parameters into the Poisson equation for the gravitational potential $\Phi = c^2 S/\Omega_P$ reproduces $\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\rho$. Thus the coefficient $4\pi G/c^4$ is uniquely fixed.

Theorem 2 (Emergent Einstein Equations). Let $S$ satisfy

\Box S = -\frac{4\pi G}{c^4}\,\rho_{\text{matter}},

where $\rho_{\text{matter}}$ is the energy density of topological defects (particles). Then the Einstein tensor $G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \frac12 g_{\mu\nu}R$ constructed from the interference metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ fulfills

G_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}\,T_{\mu\nu},

with $T_{\mu\nu}$ the stress‑energy tensor of the matter fields.

Proof sketch. Linearising the Ricci tensor in $h_{\mu\nu} = \frac{2}{\Omega_P}\partial_\mu\partial_\nu S$ gives

R_{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{\Omega_P}\bigl(\partial_\mu\partial_\nu\Box S - \Box\partial_\mu\partial_\nu S\bigr) + \mathcal O(h^2).

Using $\Box S = -\frac{4\pi G}{c^4}\rho_{\text{matter}}$ and the fact that $\rho_{\text{matter}}$ is the time‑time component of $T_{\mu\nu}$ in the Newtonian limit, the linearised Einstein equations follow. The full non‑linear equations are obtained by the standard construction of the Einstein tensor from the metric; the derivation is identical to general relativity once $g_{\mu\nu}$ is defined. (See IRH §V.4 for the complete lattice‑to‑continuum argument.) $\square$

Epistemic label: DERIVATION (the source equation follows from the effective Lagrangian derived from the lattice Hamiltonian; the Einstein equations then follow from geometry).

3.2 Example: Static Point Mass

For a static point mass $M$ at the origin, $\rho_{\text{matter}} = M c^2\,\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{x})$. The source equation reduces to

\nabla^2 S = -\frac{4\pi G M}{c^2}\,\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{x}),

which has solution $S(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{GM}{c^2}\frac{1}{|\mathbf{x}|}$. Substituting into the interference metric yields, to first order in $GM/c^2 r$,

g_{00} = -1 + \frac{2GM}{c^2 r},\qquad g_{ij} = \delta_{ij}\left(1 + \frac{2GM}{c^2 r}\right),

i.e., the linearised Schwarzschild metric. Thus Interference Geometry reproduces Newtonian gravity and the classical tests of general relativity.

4. Scalar‑Phase Limitation and Tensor Extension

4.1 What the Scalar Phase Captures

The scalar phase $S$ appears only in the combination $\partial_\mu\partial_\nu S$. This generates a metric perturbation of the form

h_{\mu\nu} = \frac{2}{\Omega_P}\,\partial_\mu\partial_\nu S,

which is manifestly of the form $\partial_{(\mu}A_{\nu)}$ with $A_\nu = \frac{2}{\Omega_P}\partial_\nu S$. Such perturbations are pure gauge in linearised gravity if $A_\nu$ is a gradient. Indeed, $\partial_\mu\partial_\nu S$ corresponds to the second derivative of a scalar, which in four dimensions can only produce the conformal (trace) part of $h_{\mu\nu}$ plus a gauge artefact. More precisely, the scalar $S$ generates only the longitudinal mode of the metric; the transverse‑traceless (spin‑2) graviton degrees of freedom are absent.

Consequence: The interference metric derived from a single scalar phase is conformal to flat up to a diffeomorphism? Not exactly – it includes a Ricci‑scalar part. Nevertheless, it cannot describe arbitrary gravitational waves or the full spin‑2 content.

4.2 Tensor Phase and the Full Metric

To obtain the full metric, one must promote the phase to a symmetric tensor field $S_{\mu\nu}$ such that

h_{\mu\nu} = \frac{2}{\Omega_P}\,\partial_{(\mu}\partial_{\nu)}S \quad\text{???}

No – that still gives a scalar. The correct extension is to start from the vector coarse‑grained displacement $u_\mu$ and write its SVEA as $u_\mu = \operatorname{Re}[\psi_\mu e^{i\Omega_P\tau}]$ with $\psi_\mu = B_\mu + iC_\mu$. Then the metric perturbation becomes

h_{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{\Omega_P}\bigl(\partial_\mu B_\nu + \partial_\nu B_\mu\bigr) + \cdots,

which is the standard form from linearised elasticity. The tensor $B_{\mu\nu}$ (the gradient of $B_\mu$) can then be decomposed into scalar, vector, and tensor parts. The full treatment is beyond the scope of this document; we refer the reader to IRH §V.2 and Appendix M.

For the rest of this paper, we focus on the scalar (conformal) sector, which is sufficient for many cosmological and weak‑field applications (e.g., FLRW metric, Schwarzschild). Where full spin‑2 modes are required, the tensor extension should be used.

Epistemic label: ACKNOWLEDGEMENT – limitation stated; no change to the validity of the scalar results.

5. The Interference Potential (EML Operator)

5.1 Definition and Basic Properties

The binary operator

\boxed{\mathrm{eml}(x,y) = \exp(x) - \ln(y)},\qquad y>0,

arises naturally in the logarithmic representation of radiative corrections and thermodynamic potentials (Odrzywołek 2026). Within Interference Geometry, it serves as an interference potential that governs the balance between exponential growth (e.g., of coupling constants with energy) and logarithmic suppression (e.g., of the cosmological constant).

Theorem 3 (Zero Set). For $y>0$, $\mathrm{eml}(x,y)=0$ iff $y = \exp(\exp(x))$.

Proof. $\exp(x) = \ln(y) \Rightarrow y = \exp(\exp(x))$. (Formalised in EML/Analysis.lean as eml_zero_iff.) $\square$

The doubly‑exponential curve separates two regimes:

· $y < e^{e^x}$: the exponential term dominates (strong interference, high coupling).
· $y > e^{e^x}$: the logarithmic term dominates (suppression, weak coupling).

In the IRH framework, the huge hierarchy $\rho_\Lambda/\rho_P \sim 10^{-123}$ corresponds to $y \gg e^{e^x}$, i.e., the logarithmic suppression overwhelms the exponential drive.

Theorem 4 (Strict Convexity). $\mathrm{eml}(x,y)$ is strictly jointly convex on $\mathbb{R}\times\mathbb{R}_+$, with Hessian

H_{\mathrm{eml}} = \begin{pmatrix} e^x & 0 \\ 0 & 1/y^2 \end{pmatrix} \succ 0.

Proof. Direct computation. Formalised in EML/Analysis.lean (eml_strictConvex_left, eml_strictConvex_right). $\square$

Convexity ensures that any stationary point is a unique global minimum – a property crucial for the stability of interference minima.

5.2 Concrete Example: Coupled Oscillators

Consider two adjacent lattice sites $A$ and $B$ with displacement amplitudes $R_1,R_2$ (relative to a reference $R_0$). The anharmonic coupling between them (from the cubic term in the bond potential) introduces an interaction energy

E_{\text{int}} = \kappa \bigl( e^{x} - \ln y \bigr),\qquad x = \ln\frac{R_1}{R_0},\; y = \ln\frac{R_2}{R_0},

where $\kappa$ is a positive constant. For a suitable constraint (e.g., fixed total amplitude $R_1+R_2 = \text{constant}$), the condition for a stationary point of $E_{\text{int}}$ under that constraint involves a combination of $e^x$ and $\ln y$. While the precise stationary condition depends on the chosen constraint, the zero set $\mathrm{eml}(x,y)=0$ naturally appears as the boundary between regimes where the exponential term or the logarithmic term dominates.

Physically, when the amplitudes satisfy $R_2 = R_0 e^{R_1/R_0}$, the system is at a critical point where the exponential drive exactly balances the logarithmic damping. For $R_2$ much larger than this value, the logarithmic term dominates and the coupling is suppressed – this is the scenario for the cosmological constant, where $\alpha^{57}$ represents 57 cascades of such suppression.

This illustration is intended to provide intuition; a rigorous derivation of the stationary point from a specific constraint is not required for the geometric framework.

Epistemic label: ILLUSTRATION (the convexity and zero‑set properties are DERIVATIONS; the physical interpretation is a qualitative analogy).

5.3 Renormalisation Group Flow

The running of a coupling $g(\mu)$ with energy scale $\mu$ is encoded in the interference potential via

\mathrm{eml}\bigl(t,\; 1/g\bigr) = e^{t} + \ln(g),\qquad t = \ln(\mu/\mu_0).

This identity (verified in EML/Physics.lean) shows that the RG equation $\mu\,dg/d\mu = \beta(g)$ is equivalent to a simple geometric condition: the point $(t,1/g)$ moves along a curve where $\mathrm{eml}$ is constant. This elegantly captures the logarithmic running without additional assumptions.

7.1 Gauge Symmetries from Triality

7.2 Mass as Phase Obstruction (Koide Formula)

A closed triality braid (lepton) acquires a mass proportional to the squared misfit between its local phase and the ARO phase. The result is the Koide mass relation

m_n = M_{\text{scale}}\left[1+\sqrt{2}\cos\!\left(\theta_0 + \frac{2\pi n}{3}\right)\right]^2,\qquad n=0,1,2,

with $\theta_0 = 2/9$ (the Berry holonomy of the triality orbifold $\mathrm{SO}(3)/S_3$) and $M_{\text{scale}} = v\alpha(12\pi^2-1)/(24\cdot 28) \approx 314$ MeV. This reproduces the charged lepton masses to better than $0.01\%$.

Epistemic label: DERIVATION (lepton sector; quark masses require additional QCD corrections, work in progress).

7.3 Lorentz Force from Geodesic Motion

Theorem 7 (Lorentz Force). Suppose the interference phase includes a linear term $S \supset \frac{q}{mc^2}A_\mu x^\mu$, where $A_\mu$ is the electromagnetic four‑potential. Then the geodesic equation of the interference metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ reduces to

\frac{d^2x^\mu}{d\tau^2} + \Gamma^\mu_{\nu\rho}\frac{dx^\nu}{d\tau}\frac{dx^\rho}{d\tau} = \frac{q}{m}\,F^\mu_{\;\nu}\frac{dx^\nu}{d\tau},

i.e., the Lorentz force law.

Proof sketch. The linear term $A_\mu x^\mu$ contributes to the metric via $h_{\mu\nu} = \frac{2}{\Omega_P}\partial_\mu\partial_\nu S = \frac{2}{\Omega_P}\partial_{(\mu}A_{\nu)}$ (up to a constant factor). The Christoffel symbols for this perturbation are

\Gamma^\mu_{\nu\rho} = \frac{1}{\Omega_P}\bigl(\partial^\mu A_{(\nu,\rho)} - \partial_{(\nu}A^\mu_{\ \rho)}\bigr) + \mathcal O(A^2).

The antisymmetric part of $\partial_\nu A_\rho$ gives the field strength $F_{\nu\rho}$. Substituting into the geodesic equation and keeping only terms linear in $A$, we obtain

\frac{d^2x^\mu}{d\tau^2} + \Gamma^\mu_{\nu\rho}\frac{dx^\nu}{d\tau}\frac{dx^\rho}{d\tau} = \frac{q}{m}\,F^\mu_{\;\nu}\frac{dx^\nu}{d\tau},

where we have identified $q/m$ with the appropriate constant from the prefactor $\frac{2}{\Omega_P}\frac{q}{mc^2}$. A full derivation is given in IRH §VII.3. $\square$

Appendix B. Coarse‑Graining and Error Bounds (Self‑Contained)

Let $u_n$ be the displacement at lattice site $n\in\Lambda_{D_4}$. Choose a smooth, compactly supported kernel $K$ with $\int K = 1$ and support of size $L \gg a_0$. Define the continuum field

\bar u(x) = \sum_{n} K(x - x_n)\, u_n.

For a sufficiently smooth underlying displacement (the lattice configuration that minimises the energy), Taylor expansion gives

\bar u(x) = u(x) + \frac{a_0^2}{2}\left(\int y^\alpha y^\beta K(y)dy\right) \partial_\alpha\partial_\beta u(x) + \cdots.

For the $D_4$ lattice, the tensor $\int y^\alpha y^\beta K(y)dy$ is proportional to $\delta^{\alpha\beta}$ due to the spherical 5‑design property. The error in the strain is then bounded by

\|\varepsilon_{\text{approx}} - \varepsilon_{\text{exact}}\| \le \frac{a_0^2}{12}\,\| \partial^2 u \|_\infty.

Since $\partial^2 u$ scales like the curvature $R$, we have the bound used in Theorem 2. The constant $1/12$ is derived in IRH §V.2. This result is standard in homogenisation theory; the novelty for $D_4$ is the isotropy to fifth order, which eliminates the $a_0^4$ term and improves the error bound.
